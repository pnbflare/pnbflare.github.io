]]> Nearly 200 Housatonic Community College (HCC) and students from Central High School gained real-world financial insights and lessons from leaders at an interactive workshop designed to prepare them to secure their financial futures.

Students engaged in an interactive discussion, surveys and Q&A on topics such as establishing smart spending practices, creating a financial plan, paying for student loans, saving on a limited budget, and diversifying investments.

The event, held during Financial Literacy Month in April, was sponsored by Patriot Bank as part of its mission to help educate the next generation on this critical topic. “Acquiring sound financial habits and making smart money decisions at a younger age will prove beneficial for a lifetime, helping young people secure their financial futures,” said Patriot Bank President Richard Muskus Jr. “Patriot Bank is encouraged that so many young people are being engaged in this topic and that Housatonic is supporting it through many initiatives, such as newly created coursework.”

A survey by Patriot found that while 54% of Housatonic students are regularly saving, 46% are not.

Speakers included Mr. Muskus; Dr. Michael Brown, HCC’s Dean of Academic Affairs; Bernard Pasierb, HCC Instructor of Business Administration; Charles Mayrick, HCC Assistant Professor of Business and Economics; and Beverly Hoppie, Director of Education and Community Engagement at the Bridgeport Neighborhood Trust. Housatonic and Patriot will present a second seminar in the fall semester as part of their collaboration in teaching financial literacy skills.

It was added this year as a business elective class after Business Administration Professors Bernard Pasierb and Mary Ann Vlahac recognized significant student interest in gaining the knowledge and skills needed to make informed lifelong financial decisions. The course teaches money management, goal setting, budgeting, saving, taxes, consumer credit, automobiles, housing, insurance, investment vehicles, retirement and estate planning.

A two-year state college, Housatonic specializes in offering STEAM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art and Math) programs for students earning their Associate in Arts and Associates in Science degrees and helps them begin their careers. ]]> Patriot Bank President Richard Muskus Jr. wrote an op-ed about the importance of financial literacy that was published in the Sunday, April 9 edition of the Stamford Advocate

 

Compared to their international peers, American youth score below average when it comes to financial literacy or understanding how to manage money and make informed financial decisions, according to the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development.

April is Financial Literacy Month, a time when our leaders in Hartford ought to evaluate how Connecticut is faring on this vital educational topic. It is also an important occasion for young people to take inventory and sharpen financial skills to better ensure their economic security now and in the future.

Financial literacy is a necessary skill in all stages of life, from planning for college to making major purchases, like a home or car, to saving for retirement. Yet only 57 percent of U.S. adults are financially literate, according to a new report by Patriot Bank, Commitment to Financial Literacy 2017.

Improving financial literacy must start with teaching the next generation the key building blocks — earning, saving and investing, protecting, spending, and borrowing — to begin securing a more stable financial future.

There are more than half a million public school students in Connecticut, yet fewer than 7 percent of its high school students are required to take a financial literacy course to graduate, earning the state an unfortunate “F” grade on the annual National Report Card produced by the Champlain College Center for Financial Literacy.

Connecticut leaves it up to the 200 individual school districts to determine what, if any, curricula to establish in this subject, unfortunately resulting in most schools not requiring personal finance instruction. In contrast, neighboring New York State incorporates personal finance in high school economics and scores a “B” on the same report card.

Financial literacy can have wide-ranging economic impacts, particularly when it comes to investing in one’s future. In Connecticut, more than half (52 percent) of adults are in debt or not saving, according to FINRA Investor Education Foundation’s Financial Capability in the United States 2016 survey, making it apparent why 53 percent say it’s difficult to cover expenses and pay bills. It also helps explain why more than half (52 percent) of the state’s parents are not saving for their children’s college education or their own retirement (55 percent).

Financial decision-making begins early in life and increases as young people gain the independence to make more important choices. A teenager may first face the challenge of balancing the allotted monthly data on a cell phone plan; later, he or she confronts the prospect of paying for college without accruing burdensome debt; as a young adult, one of the first major financial decisions may be signing the lease on an apartment or buying a car.

Young people must be equipped with the skills to make smart decisions that can help them avoid ruinous debt or other financial hardships that can have lifelong consequences.

The emerging Generation Z, born after Millennials, roughly between the mid-1990s and 2010s, will be the largest growing group in the workplace and marketplace in the next five years. In the coming decades as well, the buying power future generations will be amplified by the “Great Transfer” of wealth, in which Baby Boomers will pass along an estimated $30 trillion to their children and grandchildren.

Teaching youth financial skills is critical to building a future with informed decision-makers, smart investors and prudent financial stewards, all key ingredients for a healthy economy.

Without public education requirements, our children’s financial knowledge largely depends upon personal experiences and lessons from parents and community organizations and it is important to support these efforts. These are important resources but they are not enough by themselves.

That is why Patriot Bank is embarking on a months-long effort to connect with community youth, college students, young adults and even veterans to amplify awareness of the critical ABC’s of financial literacy. It’s never too early or late to begin getting your own financial house in order.

Every individual needs to be financially literate. As parents, educators and community members, it’s our responsibility to impart lessons and share experiences that empower the next generation to become knowledgeable consumers and strong economic decision makers.
